## Abstract
This study explored mechanisms underlying suicidal ideation among Japanese adolescents. We surveyed 2,264 university students (53.93% female) using a multidimensional assessment scale comprising 12 subscales measuring interpersonal relationships and adaptation, alongside questions about suicidal ideation frequency. We constructed a structural equation model using DirectLiNGAM, creating a network diagram linking 12 factors to suicidal ideation. Hierarchical clustering based on relative edge contributions identified characteristic factors among individuals with suicidal ideation. High-risk cluster analysis revealed that low self-acceptance, deteriorating friendships, and physical complaints including sleep deprivation significantly contributed to suicidal ideation. Subsequently, focusing exclusively on adolescents with frequent suicidal ideation, we performed hierarchical clustering on the network model, identifying six distinct clusters. Five exhibited distinctive factor patterns: one linked developmental disorder tendencies, bullying experiences, emotional instability, and low self-acceptance, while another connected decreased learning motivation and diminished vitality. One cluster showed no characteristic features, suggesting additional unmeasured factors. Although suicide involves complex interactions among multiple factors, our findings demonstrate five identifiable patterns within this complexity. Rather than a uniform mechanism, suicidal ideation arises through diverse pathways, with different individuals following distinct factor chains. Tailoring educational and psychological interventions to these specific risk factor patterns may significantly enhance prevention effectiveness.
